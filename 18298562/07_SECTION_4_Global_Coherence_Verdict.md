Section 4 — Global Coherence Verdict

The evaluation of global coherence rests on a single criterion: whether the decision, once enacted, converges toward stability without requiring continuous compensation. This criterion is structural and applies uniformly to any decision treated as a systemic object.

Applied to the dissolution announced on 9 June 2024, the criterion yields a clear result.

The dissolution did not converge toward a stable configuration. Across institutional, collective, executive, communicational, and temporal domains, the decision generated a persistent need for corrective mechanisms. Governance continuity depended on exceptional procedures, repeated reconfigurations, and negotiated tolerances rather than on structural alignment.

Corrective pressure did not diminish over time. Stability, when present, was provisional and contingent. The trajectory did not exhibit the signature of global coherence: reduction of tension, decrease of fragmentation, and consolidation of collective orientation.

This is sufficient to establish the verdict.

The dissolution is locally coherent at the point of decision, but globally incoherent as a systemic object. Its persistence required sustained effort rather than structural convergence, and its effects did not resolve into a coherent long-term trajectory compatible with the system’s capacity for maintenance.

This verdict does not assess legitimacy, responsibility, or intention. It indicates a mismatch between local validity and global constraints.
