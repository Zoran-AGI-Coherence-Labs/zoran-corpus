Section 2 — Methodology for Global Coherence Evaluation

Evaluating global coherence requires a methodological shift. Unlike local coherence, which can be assessed at the moment of decision through legal, logical, or procedural criteria, global coherence can only be evaluated through the behavior of the decision once it is embedded in a system and exposed to time.

The methodology treats the decision as a systemic object rather than as an isolated act. The object under evaluation is not the intention of the decision-maker, nor the normative validity of the act, but the trajectory generated by the decision across multiple states.

The evaluation follows three principles.

First, the analysis is state-based. The decision is examined across a finite and explicit set of states: reception by institutional actors, collective reconfiguration, execution and governance, diffusion through media and networks, and temporal evolution. Each state represents a potential point of coherence preservation or loss. A failure at any single state is sufficient to destabilize the global trajectory.

Second, the analysis is non-causal and non-counterfactual. The methodology does not attempt to identify causes, assign responsibility, or speculate about alternative decisions. It observes what occurs, not what could have occurred. The criterion is maintainability: whether the decision, as enacted, converges toward stability or requires continuous compensation to persist.

Third, the analysis is temporal. Global coherence cannot be inferred from short-term outcomes or immediate reactions. It is evaluated through the accumulation or dissipation of corrective mechanisms over time. A globally coherent decision tends to reduce corrective pressure as it stabilizes. A globally incoherent decision tends to generate increasing correction, fragmentation, and exceptional measures.

Operationally, the evaluation relies on observable indicators rather than subjective assessments. These indicators include: repeated governance reconfigurations, use of exceptional procedures to maintain continuity, persistent fragmentation of collective alignment, and the absence of convergence toward a stable configuration.

No quantitative threshold is imposed. The methodology is diagnostic rather than predictive. It identifies patterns of coherence loss rather than computing absolute scores.
