Global Incoherence of the Dissolution of the French National Assembly
Demonstrated by Systemic and Temporal Evaluation

Abstract

This white paper evaluates the dissolution of the French National Assembly announced on 9 June 2024 as a systemic object unfolding across institutional, collective, communicational, and temporal states.

A strict separation is introduced between local coherence and global coherence. Local coherence evaluates admissibility at the point of decision (clarity, legal admissibility, immediate non-contradiction). Global coherence evaluates admissibility over time (reception, execution, diffusion, corrective load, and convergence toward stability).

Local coherence for the act has been established independently (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18298563). This paper therefore does not reassess the local validity of the gesture. It evaluates the dissolution at the global level and demonstrates global incoherence through observable traces: persistent fragmentation, repeated corrective mechanisms, and the absence of convergence toward a stable configuration over time.

The work is non-partisan and non-normative. It assigns no intent, no responsibility, and proposes no counterfactual alternatives. Its purpose is to make explicit a two-level coherence framework that generalizes to governance, boards, and organizational decisions.

Introduction

The dissolution of the French National Assembly announced on 9 June 2024 has been widely assessed in terms of legality, political calculation, and democratic intent. These evaluations address an important but limited question: whether the act was locally coherent at the moment it was taken.

This paper addresses a different question, operating at a different level.

A decision may be locally coherent and yet globally incoherent. Local coherence concerns internal consistency at the point of decision: clarity of intent, legal admissibility, and immediate non-contradiction. Global coherence concerns the behavior of the same decision once it becomes an object in the world: its reception, execution, diffusion, capacity to stabilize, and behavior over time.

The local coherence of the dissolution has been established independently (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18298563). The present work therefore does not revisit the admissibility of the gesture itself. It evaluates the dissolution as a systemic object and demonstrates its global incoherence by systemic and temporal evaluation.

The dissolution constitutes a rare empirical object: an irreversible public decision, constitutionally admissible, massively diffused, collectively evaluated by institutions, media, and citizens, and followed by observable consequences over an extended period. The temporal dimension is therefore not speculative but empirical.

This work does not judge legitimacy, ideology, or strategic intent. It applies a state-based and time-based audit to determine whether the dissolution, taken as a whole, converges toward stability without requiring continuous compensation.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 1 fixes the distinction between local and global coherence. Section 2 defines the methodology for global coherence evaluation. Section 3 treats the dissolution as a systemic object across its states. Section 4 states the global coherence verdict. Section 5 articulates the two-DOI framework and clarifies how local validity and global admissibility can be jointly true without contradiction.

Section 1 — Local and Global Coherence

Local coherence and global coherence do not measure the same property, and they do not operate at the same level. Confusing them is a category error that underlies many persistent misunderstandings in the evaluation of public and organizational decisions.

Local coherence refers to the internal consistency of a decision at the point where it is taken. It concerns the act itself: clarity of intent, logical non-contradiction, legal admissibility, and immediate intelligibility for the decision-maker. A locally coherent decision does not negate its own premises at the moment of action.

Global coherence refers to the behavior of the decision once it becomes an object in the world. It concerns the trajectory of the decision after it is taken: how it is received, how it propagates, how it is executed, how it is compensated, and how it evolves over time. A globally coherent decision is one that can be absorbed by the system in which it occurs and that converges toward stability without requiring continuous correction.

Local coherence is a necessary condition. It is never a sufficient condition.

A decision that is locally incoherent collapses immediately. A decision that is locally coherent may persist long enough to appear valid, even while it progressively destabilizes the system that must carry it. The distinction is therefore structural: local coherence governs admissibility at the point of action, while global coherence governs maintainability across time.

The relationship between the two levels is asymmetric. Global coherence presupposes local coherence, but local coherence does not guarantee global coherence. The passage from one level to the other is mediated by the system itself: its structure, its capacity for absorption, its tolerance to fragmentation, and its ability to integrate change without contradiction.

This distinction explains why many decisions are defended as rational, legal, or necessary, yet still produce instability, fatigue, and repeated corrective measures. The failure does not originate in the act, but in the mismatch between the act and the system’s capacity to sustain it as a whole.

In the case examined here, the local coherence of the dissolution has been established independently. This paper brackets the evaluation of the act at the moment it was taken and evaluates exclusively the global coherence of the dissolution as it unfolds across states and time.

Section 2 — Methodology for Global Coherence Evaluation

Evaluating global coherence requires a methodological shift. Unlike local coherence, which can be assessed at the moment of decision through legal, logical, or procedural criteria, global coherence can only be evaluated through the behavior of the decision once it is embedded in a system and exposed to time.

The methodology treats the decision as a systemic object rather than as an isolated act. The object under evaluation is not the intention of the decision-maker, nor the normative validity of the act, but the trajectory generated by the decision across multiple states.

The evaluation follows three principles.

First, the analysis is state-based. The decision is examined across a finite and explicit set of states: reception by institutional actors, collective reconfiguration, execution and governance, diffusion through media and networks, and temporal evolution. Each state represents a potential point of coherence preservation or loss. A failure at any single state is sufficient to destabilize the global trajectory.

Second, the analysis is non-causal and non-counterfactual. The methodology does not attempt to identify causes, assign responsibility, or speculate about alternative decisions. It observes what occurs, not what could have occurred. The criterion is maintainability: whether the decision, as enacted, converges toward stability or requires continuous compensation to persist.

Third, the analysis is temporal. Global coherence cannot be inferred from short-term outcomes or immediate reactions. It is evaluated through the accumulation or dissipation of corrective mechanisms over time. A globally coherent decision tends to reduce corrective pressure as it stabilizes. A globally incoherent decision tends to generate increasing correction, fragmentation, and exceptional measures.

Operationally, the evaluation relies on observable indicators rather than subjective assessments. These indicators include: repeated governance reconfigurations, use of exceptional procedures to maintain continuity, persistent fragmentation of collective alignment, and the absence of convergence toward a stable configuration.

No quantitative threshold is imposed. The methodology is diagnostic rather than predictive. It identifies patterns of coherence loss rather than computing absolute scores.

Section 3 — The Dissolution as a Systemic Object

Once enacted, the dissolution ceased to be an act and became an object circulating within a complex system. From that moment onward, its coherence could no longer be evaluated at the level of intent or legality, but only through its effects on the structures required to carry it.

As a systemic object, the dissolution entered simultaneously into several domains: institutional architecture, political organization, media ecosystems, network diffusion, and collective temporal dynamics. Each domain imposed constraints that were not present at the point of decision. Global coherence therefore depended on compatibility with these constraints as a whole.

At the institutional level, the dissolution triggered a reconfiguration of parliamentary representation. Elections were held according to constitutional procedures, yet the resulting configuration did not converge toward a stable majority. The Assembly emerged fragmented, with no bloc capable of sustaining governance without external or exceptional support. Fragmentation persisted beyond the electoral cycle.

At the collective level, political actors adapted locally to the new configuration, forming tactical oppositions and conditional supports. These adaptations allowed the system to continue functioning but did not generate a coherent collective orientation. Alignment was replaced by negotiation, and negotiation by repeated arbitration. The collective dimension therefore operated in a compensatory mode rather than a stabilizing one.

At the level of execution, the trajectory exhibited discontinuity: successive governments formed and dissolved within a short period; exceptional procedures were invoked to bypass structural deadlock; corrective actions addressed immediate blockages but did not reduce the overall corrective load. Execution became dependent on procedural force rather than structural convergence.

At the level of diffusion, narrative coherence was not preserved across channels. While institutional communication remained formally consistent, interpretations diverged and re-fragmented as new corrective events occurred. Diffusion amplified fragmentation rather than resolving it.

The temporal dimension consolidates these observations. Over an extended period, the dissolution did not generate a trajectory of decreasing correction and increasing stability. Persistence was achieved through sustained effort rather than through coherence.

Section 4 — Global Coherence Verdict

The evaluation of global coherence rests on a single criterion: whether the decision, once enacted, converges toward stability without requiring continuous compensation. This criterion is structural and applies uniformly to any decision treated as a systemic object.

Applied to the dissolution announced on 9 June 2024, the criterion yields a clear result.

The dissolution did not converge toward a stable configuration. Across institutional, collective, executive, communicational, and temporal domains, the decision generated a persistent need for corrective mechanisms. Governance continuity depended on exceptional procedures, repeated reconfigurations, and negotiated tolerances rather than on structural alignment.

Corrective pressure did not diminish over time. Stability, when present, was provisional and contingent. The trajectory did not exhibit the signature of global coherence: reduction of tension, decrease of fragmentation, and consolidation of collective orientation.

This is sufficient to establish the verdict.

The dissolution is locally coherent at the point of decision, but globally incoherent as a systemic object. Its persistence required sustained effort rather than structural convergence, and its effects did not resolve into a coherent long-term trajectory compatible with the system’s capacity for maintenance.

This verdict does not assess legitimacy, responsibility, or intention. It indicates a mismatch between local validity and global constraints.

Section 5 — Articulation of Local and Global Coherence (Two-DOI Framework)

Local coherence evaluates admissibility at the point of action. Global coherence evaluates admissibility over time. They operate at different levels and answer different questions.

Local coherence establishes whether the act can be taken without negating its own premises. The local coherence of the dissolution has been established independently (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18298563). That evaluation validates the admissibility of the gesture within its immediate framework. It cannot, by construction, evaluate the behavior of the decision once embedded in the system.

Global coherence evaluates the same object as it unfolds in reality. It asks whether the decision can be absorbed, stabilized, and maintained without continuous compensation. This paper demonstrates that the dissolution fails this test and is therefore globally incoherent.

The relationship is asymmetric. Local coherence is necessary; it is never sufficient. A decision may satisfy local coherence and fail global coherence without contradiction.

The two DOI records therefore operate together as a unified evaluative system. The local coherence DOI establishes admissibility at the point of action. The global coherence record establishes admissibility as a trajectory over time. Neither supersedes the other. Each answers a question the other cannot.

Conclusion

The dissolution of the French National Assembly announced on 9 June 2024 satisfies the conditions of local coherence: it was constitutionally admissible, internally consistent, and non-contradictory at the point of decision.

When evaluated as a systemic object unfolding across institutional, collective, communicational, and temporal states, it fails to meet the conditions of global coherence. Its trajectory required continuous compensation and did not converge toward a stable configuration compatible with the system’s capacity for maintenance.

The result is structural, not ideological. Local validity does not guarantee global admissibility. Time does not correct incoherence; it reveals whether corrective pressure converges toward stability or accumulates as debt.

Separating local coherence from global coherence allows local validity to be acknowledged without denying global failure, and incoherence to be identified without attributing fault.
