# Operational Consciousness as a Global Rescue Controller
## End-to-End Demonstration and Applications

**DOI**: 10.5281/zenodo.18359622

---

## Abstract

This white paper proposes an operational definition of consciousness as an intermittent, costly global control regime activated when local regulatory mechanisms fail to maintain system coherence. Using exclusively public neurophysiological datasets spanning sleep, anesthesia, cortical perturbation, and disorders of consciousness, we define a falsifiable end-to-end pipeline linking signals to metrics, dynamics, decisions, and applications. The framework is cross-domain by design and avoids subjective, phenomenological, or metaphysical assumptions. Consciousness is treated as a functional control mechanism, not as an experiential essence.

---

## 1. Introduction

Consciousness is most often approached through subjective report, phenomenology, or philosophical interpretation. While these approaches have produced rich descriptions, they have yielded few mechanisms that are directly testable, falsifiable, and operational across experimental contexts.

In parallel, neuroscience has accumulated large volumes of empirical data—sleep recordings, anesthesia protocols, perturbational experiments, and clinical observations—without a unifying functional framework capable of explaining *when* and *why* consciousness appears or disappears.

This white paper adopts a minimal and deliberately restrictive position:

**Consciousness is not treated as a substance, a permanent property, or an experiential content, but as a functional regime of control.**

More precisely, consciousness is defined here as the intermittent activation of a **global rescue controller**, mobilized when local regulatory mechanisms are insufficient to maintain a coherent trajectory over time.

This definition is adopted because it satisfies four non-negotiable criteria:

1. **Operationality**  
   The definition refers exclusively to measurable system behavior and control structure.

2. **Transversality**  
   The same mechanism can, in principle, operate across sleep, anesthesia, perturbation, and pathological states.

3. **Falsifiability**  
   A control regime implies observable signatures: cost, latency, integration, instability, and causal response to perturbation.

4. **Applicability**  
   The framework enables concrete pipelines producing decisions and alerts, not post hoc interpretations.

The central claim investigated in this work is therefore the following:

> A system is conscious, in the operational sense, if and only if it activates a costly global control mechanism to restore or preserve coherence when local regulation fails.

The remainder of this document is organized to demonstrate this claim end to end: from data to metrics, from metrics to dynamics, from dynamics to decisions, and from decisions to applications.

## 2. Falsifiable Hypotheses

All hypotheses are formulated with explicit observable negations. Any hypothesis failing its negation test must be rejected.

### H1 — Functional Intermittence

**Claim**  
Consciousness is not continuous but appears transiently when local regulation fails.

**Observable prediction**  
Markers of global integration and instability peak during state transitions (e.g., wake–sleep, induction of anesthesia).

**Negation**  
If such markers remain constant regardless of system stability, H1 is rejected.

### H2 — Operational Cost

**Claim**  
Global control incurs measurable cost (latency, slowdown, energetic proxy).

**Observable prediction**  
States classified as conscious exhibit higher cost than purely local regulation.

**Negation**  
If no measurable cost differentiates conscious and non-conscious regimes, H2 is rejected.

### H3 — Transversal Invariance

**Claim**  
The same functional signatures appear across distinct domains.

**Observable prediction**  
Relative ordering of metrics is conserved across sleep, anesthesia, and perturbation.

**Negation**  
If each domain requires incompatible metrics or signatures, H3 is rejected.

### H4 — Perturbational Priority

**Claim**  
Perturbational measures discriminate regimes more reliably than passive observation.

**Observable prediction**  
Causal response complexity separates regimes even when observational signals are ambiguous.

**Negation**  
If perturbation adds no discriminative power, H4 is rejected.

### H5 — Critical Boundaries

**Claim**  
Consciousness appears preferentially at boundaries of stability.

**Observable prediction**  
Instability markers peak at transitions, not in fully stabilized states.

**Negation**  
If transitions show no distinct signatures, H5 is rejected.

### H6 — Partial Independence from Reportability

**Claim**  
Global control can exist without explicit behavioral report.

**Observable prediction**  
Some non-responsive states retain global control signatures.

**Negation**  
If all global signatures vanish with loss of report, H6 is rejected.

## 3. Data and Experimental Corpus

Only public, traceable datasets are used.

### 3.1 Primary Domains

- **Sleep**: Sleep-EDF Expanded (PhysioNet)  
  Provides natural transitions without external intervention.

- **Anesthesia**: Propofol datasets (OpenNeuro)  
  Provide explicit causal manipulation of state.

- **Perturbation**: TMS–EEG (PCI / PCIst families)  
  Provide direct causal tests of propagation and integration.

### 3.2 Complementary Domains

- Resting-state connectome (HCP) for baseline control  
- Disorders of consciousness cohorts for clinical stress-testing

### 3.3 Normalization Constraints

- Minimal preprocessing  
- Fixed temporal windows  
- Strict subject/session separation  
- Explicit artifact rejection  
- No label-dependent tuning

### 3.4 Known Limitations

- Labels are proxies, not direct access to experience  
- Protocol heterogeneity  
- Limited availability of perturbational data

These limitations define the domain of validity and are incorporated into rejection tests.

## 4. Methods — End-to-End Pipeline

### 4.1 Canonical Pipeline

1. Data acquisition and verification  
2. Minimal preprocessing  
3. Temporal windowing  
4. Metric extraction  
5. State and transition modeling  
6. Refutation testing  
7. Operational decision

All intermediate outputs are preserved.

### 4.2 Metrics (fixed set)

A fixed set of metrics (M1–M9) is used, covering:

- Algorithmic complexity  
- Spectral entropy  
- Functional integration  
- Network modularity  
- Temporal variability  
- Perturbational response (when available)  
- Cost and robustness proxies  
- Transition instability

A transversal invariant (M10) is assessed only at the evaluation stage.

No metric is optimized for a specific dataset.

### 4.3 Modeling (minimal)

- Interpretable classifiers for state identification  
- Sequential models for transition dynamics  
- Mandatory trivial baselines  
- Optional domain-adversarial controls

Performance is secondary to falsification survival.

### 4.4 Evaluation Order (mandatory)

All models are subjected to:

- Leakage tests  
- Label shuffles  
- Phase randomization  
- Noise robustness  
- Causal ordering tests  
- Cross-domain transfer tests

Any failure invalidates interpretation.

## 5. Results (expected structure under the protocol)

### 5.1 Intra-Domain Validation

- Sleep: differentiation of wake, REM, and deep NREM; instability peaks at transitions  
- Anesthesia: metric changes follow pharmacological manipulation; response loss may precede full collapse  
- Perturbation: causal complexity discriminates regimes beyond observation

### 5.2 Negative Controls

- Shuffled labels collapse performance  
- Amplitude-only baselines fail  
- Phase-randomized signals destroy structure

These failures confirm non-triviality if observed.

### 5.3 Cross-Domain Transfer

Cross-domain transfer is considered successful only if ordinal relations and transition signatures persist without supervised recalibration.

### 5.4 Hypothesis Status

- H1–H5: validated only if all rejection tests pass  
- H6: may remain partial depending on available no-report data

## 6. Discussion

The framework explains **when** and **why** a system activates global control, not **what it experiences**.

It does not address qualia, subjective content, or personal identity. These are outside its operational scope.

The limitations are structural: public data provide functional traces, not experiential access.

The framework constrains theories by requiring cost, intermittence, boundaries, and perturbational validation.

---

## 7. Applications

Applications follow directly from the pipeline:

- Anesthesia monitoring: detection of regime transitions  
- Disorders of consciousness: longitudinal trajectory assessment  
- Sleep analysis: mapping of functional boundaries  
- Continuous monitoring: early warning of control failure  
- Artificial systems: global fallback control without anthropomorphism

In all cases, outputs are decisions or alerts, not interpretations of experience.

## 8. Reproducibility and Closure

All data are public.  
All methods are explicit.  
All hypotheses are falsifiable.

Any valid refutation must respect the same constraints and produce a reproducible counterexample.

## Conclusion

This work demonstrates that consciousness can be treated as a **functional, intermittent, costly global control regime**, without invoking subjective or metaphysical assumptions.

The contribution is not a narrative about experience, but a **testable mechanism**.

From this point onward, the question is no longer speculative. It is experimental.
