Le futur cohérent d’un passé incohérent
Trajectoires admissibles après une dissolution globalement incohérente

Abstract

This white paper evaluates admissible future trajectories after a globally incoherent decision, without prediction and without prescription.

It starts from two established results in the series: (1) the act of dissolution is locally coherent at the point of decision, and (2) the dissolution, as a real object unfolding across institutional, collective, diffusion, and temporal states, is globally incoherent.

The central premise is that after global incoherence is observed, the future is not an open space of choices but a constrained space of admissibility. Futures are evaluated by a single structural criterion: whether corrective pressure converges toward stability or accumulates as permanent compensation.

The analysis proceeds by consolidating observed consequences to date at the national and European levels, using them as boundary conditions, then classifying admissible future trajectories into a closed typology.

This paper completes the trilogy act–object–future by moving from local admissibility, to global admissibility, to admissible futures under constraint.

Introduction

This white paper addresses a question that can only be posed once two evaluations have already been completed.

First, the local coherence of a decision must be evaluated at the point where it is taken. Second, the global coherence of the same decision must be evaluated as it unfolds as a real object in the system. In this series, the dissolution of the French National Assembly announced on 9 June 2024 has been treated through these two levels: the act was locally coherent, while the dissolution as an object was demonstrated to be globally incoherent.

This third paper starts from that result. It does not re-evaluate the act, and it does not re-argue the global incoherence verdict. It treats the present as already constrained by what has been necessary to maintain continuity so far.

The premise is strict: after a globally incoherent decision, the future of a system is no longer open. It is constrained. Certain trajectories become impossible, others become increasingly costly, and only a limited subset remains admissible without contradiction or permanent compensation.

The purpose of this paper is therefore not to propose reforms or scenarios, and not to forecast events. It evaluates admissibility. A future is considered coherent if corrective pressure diminishes, convergence toward stability appears, and continuity can be maintained without exceptional measures becoming permanent.

The analysis is limited to the national and European levels insofar as these levels are directly coupled to the dissolution and its observed consequences. Broader international effects are excluded because they are either indirect or not yet structurally observable without causal speculation.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 1 consolidates consequences already observed to date as boundary conditions. Section 2 derives a closed typology of admissible futures under these constraints. Section 3 concludes by stating the structural implication: once incoherence has occurred, the relevant question is no longer what is desirable, but what can still hold.

Section 1 — Observed Consequences to Date
National and European Levels

This section consolidates consequences that are already observable. Its purpose is not interpretation, causality, or judgment. It establishes boundary conditions from which future trajectories must proceed.

National level

The dominant observable consequence is persistent institutional fragmentation. The post-dissolution parliamentary configuration did not converge toward a stable majority capable of sustaining governance through regular mechanisms alone. Fragmentation persisted beyond the electoral moment and became a structural condition of the present.

Governance therefore operated in a compensatory regime. Continuity was maintained through repeated reconfigurations, negotiated tolerances, and the normalization of exceptional procedures. These mechanisms prevented collapse, but they did not reduce corrective pressure over time. Stability, when present, remained provisional and contingent.

A second consequence is the normalization of exception. Procedures designed as safeguards became routine instruments of continuity. This indicates accommodation to incoherence rather than convergence toward coherence: the system functions, but at sustained cost in tension, complexity, and interpretability.

A third consequence is observable fatigue of decision cycles. This is not treated as sentiment. It is treated as repeated reopening of issues, extended arbitration cycles, and persistent reliance on corrective narratives to sustain continuity. Decisions take longer, require broader justification, and remain more fragile to perturbation.

Taken together, these elements establish that the system has absorbed incoherence, but only by internalizing it as debt: coherence is maintained through compensation rather than through structural convergence.

European level

The European level is not a separate domain but a coupled extension. France is structurally engaged in European institutional cycles, which presuppose a minimum continuity of representation and initiative. Domestic incoherence therefore produces direct constraints at this level.

A first consequence is reduced coherence of representation: the capacity to carry stable positions, sustain initiatives, and commit over multi-year horizons is weakened when domestic governance depends on short-term compensation.

A second consequence is temporal desynchronization. European decision-making operates on medium and long cycles, while domestic governance under compensatory regimes tends toward short stabilization loops. This mismatch introduces friction between national political temporality and European institutional temporality.

A third consequence is constrained initiative. When a system consumes institutional energy to sustain internal continuity, it reduces the margin for sustained external engagement without necessarily producing visible failure. Participation persists; strategic continuity becomes harder to maintain.

Boundary of analysis

These consequences are sufficient to constrain future evaluation. They show that incoherence is not exhausted by the past event; it is structuring the present. The future must therefore be evaluated as constrained admissibility rather than as open possibility.

Section 2 — Typology of Admissible Futures

After a globally incoherent decision, admissible futures are those that do not push corrective pressure beyond the system’s capacity for maintenance. The typology below is closed relative to the boundary conditions established in Section 1.

Category I — Persistent incoherence (compensatory continuation)

In this trajectory, the system continues to function through ongoing compensation. Fragmentation persists, exceptional procedures remain normalized, and stability remains provisional. Corrective pressure does not diminish; it becomes routine.

This trajectory is admissible in the short to medium term because it preserves continuity. Its structural cost is coherence debt accumulation. Over time, margins narrow and sensitivity to shocks increases.

Category II — Abrupt reconfiguration (discontinuous resolution)

In this trajectory, the system exits the compensatory regime through a discontinuity that concentrates correction into a short window. The defining feature is not the political form but the structural mechanism: coherence is restored by break rather than convergence.

This trajectory is admissible only if the system can absorb discontinuity without cascading failure. Its risk profile is high: coherence may be restored quickly, but the cost is concentrated and hard to control.

Category III — Minimal coherent recomposition (constrained stabilization)

In this trajectory, the system reduces incoherence without rupture. Corrective pressure gradually diminishes as regular mechanisms replace exceptional ones. Convergence is slow, bounded, and compatible with the system’s current structure.

This is the only admissible trajectory that reduces coherence debt over time. It does not return the system to a prior state; it settles into a new equilibrium of sufficient stability.

Category IV — Non-admissible degradation (exhaustion beyond maintenance)

In this trajectory, corrective pressure exceeds capacity. Compensation loses effectiveness and instability accelerates. This is not an admissible future but a failure mode. It is included only to mark the boundary.

Comparative statement

Only Categories I–III are admissible. Among them, only Category III reduces corrective pressure over time. Category I preserves continuity at increasing cost. Category II restores coherence at the cost of discontinuity risk. Category III restores coherence within constraint.

Section 3 — Conclusion

This paper evaluated the future after an incoherent past by treating the present as already constrained by observable consequences.

The key result is structural: after global incoherence is observed, the future is no longer open. It is bounded by what has already been required to maintain continuity. Admissible trajectories are therefore those that do not escalate corrective pressure beyond maintenance capacity.

Within these constraints, only a limited set of futures is admissible. Persistent incoherence preserves continuity at increasing cost. Abrupt reconfiguration restores coherence through discontinuity at high risk. Minimal coherent recomposition is the only trajectory that reduces corrective pressure over time.

This work does not prescribe action, assign responsibility, or propose reforms. It establishes admissibility. It completes the trilogy by moving from the act, to the object, to the constrained futures that follow.

Appendix — Method Note

Method Note

This paper uses a constraint-based approach.

1) Consolidate observed consequences to date as boundary conditions.
2) Define admissibility by maintainability: corrective pressure must converge or remain bounded.
3) Classify futures into a closed typology under those constraints.
4) Exclude predictions, counterfactuals, and causal attribution.

FAQ

Is this a prediction?
No. This work evaluates admissibility under constraints. It does not forecast events.

Is this prescriptive?
No. It does not propose reforms or policy choices. It classifies futures by maintainability.

Why include Europe?
Because European constraints are structurally coupled to national governance and are directly constrained by domestic incoherence.

Why exclude broader international effects?
Because they are indirect and would require causal speculation, which this work avoids.

Glossary

Local coherence
Admissibility of an act at the point of decision (clarity, legal admissibility, immediate non-contradiction).

Global coherence
Admissibility of a decision as a trajectory once embedded in a system over time (reception, execution, diffusion, time).

Admissible future
A trajectory that can be maintained without escalating compensation beyond maintenance capacity.

Compensation
Corrective mechanisms used to sustain continuity despite incoherence.

Coherence debt
Accumulated corrective load required to maintain a trajectory that does not converge toward stability.
