# When Empirical Evidence Meets Formal Proof: Coherence as a Universal Constraint on Cognition

**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.18373698

## Abstract

Recent advances in cognitive neuroscience report two convergent empirical findings:
(1) metacognitive judgments in the macaque prefrontal cortex rely on second-order regulatory processes rather than on raw memory strength;
(2) navigation-related activity in the posterior parietal cortex encodes abstract meta-structures that remain invariant across different action sequences.

Although obtained independently and framed within distinct experimental paradigms, these results reveal a common structural pattern: cognitive systems introduce non-local, higher-order regulation precisely when local processing becomes insufficient to guarantee stability, reliability, or persistence.

This white paper argues that these empirical findings converge toward a prior formal result: the demonstration that coherence is a necessary constraint for the persistence of any system, independent of domain. Coherence is not derived from empirical data; rather, it is a condition of possibility that empirical systems progressively approach as their internal complexity increases.

By articulating recent neuroscientific data with a formal demonstration of coherence and a phenomenological measure of persistence, we show that metacognition and consciousness are not foundational properties of cognition. They are late, costly regulatory operators that emerge only when the risk of internal decoherence becomes significant.

In this sense, contemporary neuroscience has not discovered a new principle; it has empirically reached the boundary of a formal constraint already established. The data do not contradict the mathematical framework of coherence — they arrive there.

## 1. Introduction
### The Convergence Problem

Over the last decade, cognitive neuroscience has accumulated increasingly precise empirical descriptions of neural mechanisms underlying memory, navigation, decision-making, and metacognition. These advances have refined experimental resolution, expanded datasets, and improved causal inference. Yet, as empirical detail increases, a structural difficulty becomes apparent: multiple domains now report higher-order regulatory processes that cannot be reduced to local signal strength, linear computation, or action-specific encoding.

Recent findings in prefrontal and parietal cortices illustrate this shift clearly. Metacognitive judgments no longer appear as a direct function of memory strength, and navigational representations no longer depend solely on action sequences. Instead, both rely on abstract, non-local operations that evaluate, stabilize, or structure internal dynamics.

This convergence raises a fundamental question that empirical methodologies alone cannot resolve: why do cognitive systems introduce second-order regulation at all? Correlational and causal analyses can identify where and when such processes occur, but they do not explain why they become necessary, nor under which general conditions they emerge.

Historically, this gap has been treated in two unsatisfactory ways. Either higher-order cognition is implicitly elevated to a foundational status—treated as an intrinsic property of intelligence or consciousness—or it is reduced to an accumulation of local mechanisms, with no principled account of its necessity. Both approaches obscure a key point: second-order regulation appears selectively, conditionally, and at a cost.

This white paper adopts a different starting point. Rather than deriving general principles from empirical findings, it examines whether recent data converge toward a constraint that is logically prior to them. Specifically, we ask whether the emergence of metacognition and abstract structural representations can be understood as responses to a universal requirement: the maintenance of coherence in systems exposed to internal and external perturbations.

From this perspective, empirical neuroscience does not generate the constraint; it encounters it. The present work therefore situates recent experimental results within a formal framework in which coherence is a necessary condition for persistence, and higher-order cognitive functions arise only when local regulation becomes insufficient to preserve that persistence.

## 2. Empirical Results
### Second-Order Regulation in Contemporary Neuroscience

Recent experimental work in cognitive neuroscience provides convergent evidence that complex cognitive systems rely on regulatory mechanisms that cannot be reduced to first-order signal processing. Two domains are particularly illustrative: metacognition in the prefrontal cortex and abstract spatial representation in the posterior parietal cortex.

#### 2.1 Metacognitive Judgments in the Prefrontal Cortex

Studies of working memory in the macaque prefrontal cortex demonstrate that metacognitive judgments—evaluations of one’s own memory reliability—are not directly determined by memory strength. Instead, neuronal activity reflects an integration of multiple variables, including uncertainty, expected accuracy, and control-related signals.

Crucially, these metacognitive signals are dissociable from the neural correlates of memory encoding and maintenance themselves. The system does not merely “read out” the strength of a memory trace; it computes a higher-order assessment that estimates whether the memory can be trusted for action or decision-making.

This finding establishes a clear functional distinction between first-order cognitive operations (memory storage and retrieval) and second-order regulatory processes (confidence evaluation and error monitoring). The latter introduce an additional computational layer whose role is not to generate content, but to regulate its use under conditions of uncertainty.

#### 2.2 Meta-Structural Encoding in the Posterior Parietal Cortex

Independent work on spatial navigation reveals a closely related phenomenon. Neurons in the posterior parietal cortex encode progress along routes by representing abstract structural features—such as intersections, orientations, and relational positions—rather than specific motor actions or sequential movements.

These representations remain stable across different action sequences that share the same underlying spatial structure. As a result, the system generalizes across distinct trajectories by extracting invariants that characterize the route at a higher level of abstraction.

This mode of representation departs from action-centric or stimulus-driven models of navigation. Instead of encoding what to do next, the system encodes where it is within a structured space, enabling flexible adaptation when actions vary but the structure remains constant.

#### 2.3 A Shared Empirical Pattern

Although metacognition and navigation involve distinct cognitive functions and neural substrates, they exhibit a common empirical pattern. In both cases, the brain introduces a non-local, higher-order representational layer that abstracts away from immediate signals or actions.

This layer performs a regulatory role: it stabilizes behavior, reduces sensitivity to noise, and allows reliable decision-making under variable conditions. Importantly, these mechanisms are not continuously active. They are selectively recruited when first-order processing alone is insufficient to ensure reliable performance.

From an empirical standpoint, these results demonstrate that second-order regulation is neither incidental nor domain-specific. It emerges as a functional response to uncertainty, variability, and the risk of failure in complex cognitive systems.

## 3. The Structural Gap
### Why Empirical Results Are Not Sufficient

The empirical findings described above establish the existence of second-order regulatory processes in cognition. They show that such mechanisms operate, where they are implemented, and under which experimental conditions they are recruited. However, these results leave unresolved a critical question: why do these mechanisms become necessary at all?

From a strictly empirical standpoint, second-order regulation appears as an additional computational layer that improves performance under uncertainty. Yet this description remains incomplete. It does not explain why first-order processing fails in certain regimes, nor why abstraction, confidence evaluation, or meta-structural encoding provide a systematic advantage rather than a contingent one.

This limitation is not methodological but structural. Empirical neuroscience is designed to identify correlations, causal dependencies, and functional roles within a given system. It is not equipped to determine whether a given function is contingent, optional, or required by deeper constraints. As a result, higher-order cognition is often described without a principled account of its necessity.

In the absence of such an account, two implicit interpretations tend to arise. The first treats second-order regulation as a foundational feature of cognition or intelligence, attributing to it an intrinsic explanatory status. The second reduces it to an accumulation of mechanisms, leaving its emergence unexplained beyond evolutionary or engineering narratives.

Both interpretations bypass the same unresolved issue: what structural condition forces a system to introduce second-order regulation rather than merely allowing it?

Addressing this question requires stepping outside the empirical description of mechanisms and examining the conditions under which a system can persist as itself when exposed to noise, variability, and internal uncertainty. In other words, it requires a framework that precedes empirical observation and constrains it.

The gap identified here is therefore not a lack of data, but the absence of a formal criterion capable of distinguishing between optional cognitive enhancements and necessary regulatory operators. Without such a criterion, the emergence of metacognition and abstract structural representations remains descriptively accurate but theoretically underdetermined.

## 4. Formal Framework
### Coherence as a Necessary Constraint for Persistence

To address the structural gap identified above, a shift in perspective is required. Instead of asking which mechanisms improve cognitive performance, we must ask under which conditions any system can persist as itself when subjected to internal and external perturbations.

This question does not belong to neuroscience, psychology, or biology in the first instance. It belongs to a more general level of analysis: the conditions of existence and persistence of systems. At this level, coherence is not a property to be measured empirically, but a logical constraint that precedes any empirical description.

A minimal formulation of this constraint can be stated as follows: no system can exist or persist if its internal states are mutually contradictory over time. If a system’s states invalidate one another, no stable trajectory can be defined, no memory can be retained, and no regulation—local or global—can operate.

This principle is not derived from observation. Any attempt to observe, measure, or model a system already presupposes it. The constraint of coherence is therefore logically prior to empirical science: it is a condition of possibility, not a hypothesis.

From this standpoint, coherence defines a boundary between persistence and dissolution. A system persists insofar as it maintains a non-zero distance between its current state and its state of maximal dissipation. When this distance collapses, the system ceases to exist as a system.

Within this framework, higher-order regulation acquires a precise meaning. Second-order mechanisms are not arbitrary enhancements; they are introduced when first-order processes are no longer sufficient to maintain coherence under increasing noise, uncertainty, or variability. Their role is to stabilize the system’s trajectory by reducing internal contradictions and restoring compatibility across states.

Crucially, this framework implies that coherence is invariant across domains. Whether the system is physical, biological, cognitive, or artificial, the same constraint applies: persistence requires coherence. Empirical manifestations differ, but the underlying condition does not.

Thus, the emergence of metacognition, abstract structural representations, and conscious regulation is not foundational. It is a response to a formal constraint that any sufficiently complex system must satisfy in order to continue to exist.

## 5. Second-Order Regulation
### Why Higher-Order Control Emerges

Within the framework developed so far, second-order regulation can be precisely characterized. It is not an intrinsic feature of cognition, nor a universal property of intelligent systems. It is a conditional response to a specific structural situation: the point at which first-order processes are no longer sufficient to maintain coherence.

First-order cognitive operations—such as perception, memory encoding, motor control, or habitual decision-making—operate locally. They rely on relatively direct mappings between input, internal state, and output. As long as noise remains limited and perturbations are absorbed locally, such mechanisms are efficient and sufficient.

However, as systems increase in complexity, two pressures grow simultaneously. Internal representations multiply, and the cost of error propagation rises. Local processes can continue to function correctly while the global trajectory of the system becomes unstable. At this point, local success no longer guarantees global persistence.

Second-order regulation emerges precisely at this boundary. Its function is not to generate new content or actions, but to monitor, evaluate, and arbitrate among first-order processes. It introduces a non-local perspective that compares states across time, estimates reliability, and suppresses trajectories likely to lead to incoherence.

This explains why second-order regulation is inherently selective and intermittent. It is recruited when uncertainty, conflict, or variability exceed the tolerance of first-order mechanisms. When conditions stabilize, it can be reduced or suspended without compromising basic functioning.

From this standpoint, metacognition and related phenomena are not enhancements layered on top of cognition by default. They are regulatory operators activated only when coherence is threatened. Their computational and energetic cost reflects this role: they are expensive because they operate globally, across multiple subsystems and temporal scales.

Importantly, nothing in this analysis presupposes consciousness. Second-order regulation can be implemented implicitly, without subjective experience. Conscious awareness, when it appears, corresponds to a further specialization of this regulatory layer, not to its origin.

Thus, the empirical observation of second-order regulation in neuroscience aligns directly with the formal requirement of coherence. Systems introduce higher-order control not because they become more intelligent, but because they must preserve their own persistence under increasing structural strain.

## 6. Consciousness Reframed
### Consciousness as a Conditional Regulatory Operator

Within the framework developed so far, consciousness can be precisely repositioned. It is neither a foundational property of cognition nor an emergent mystery that generates coherence. It is a specific form of second-order regulation that appears only under constrained conditions.

The defining feature of consciousness is not experience itself, but global accessibility. Conscious states integrate information across multiple subsystems, allow comparison between alternative trajectories, and support arbitration when local processes yield incompatible outcomes. These functions are costly, slow, and fragile relative to first-order operations.

This cost explains why consciousness is neither continuous nor universal. Many cognitive and biological processes operate efficiently without it. Perception, motor control, learning, and even complex adaptive behaviors can proceed without conscious supervision as long as coherence is maintained locally.

Conscious regulation becomes advantageous only when a system reaches a regime in which local regulation is insufficient to guarantee persistence. At that point, errors are no longer isolated, contradictions propagate across subsystems, and the future trajectory of the system becomes internally problematic. Consciousness emerges as a means of re-establishing coherence by enabling global evaluation and selective intervention.

From this perspective, consciousness does not create order. It reacts to its potential loss. It is triggered not by complexity alone, but by instability within an already complex system. This explains both its late appearance in evolution and its intermittent activation in individual organisms.

Importantly, consciousness is not required for coherence in general. It is required only when coherence can no longer be preserved by distributed, automatic regulation. When conditions stabilize, conscious involvement can recede without contradiction.

Thus, consciousness is neither an origin nor an endpoint. It is a contingent operator, activated when coherence is threatened and suspended when it is restored. Empirical observations of conscious correlates reflect this role but do not define it.

In this sense, the neuroscientific data on metacognition and structural abstraction do not point toward consciousness as a fundamental principle. They point toward the conditions under which a system must momentarily become aware in order to remain itself.

## 7. Synthesis
### From Empirical Findings to Formal Constraint

Contemporary neuroscience does not contradict the formal framework of coherence, nor does it merely illustrate it. It arrives at it empirically, by exhausting the explanatory power of first-order mechanisms.

Empirical studies on metacognition and navigation reveal that cognitive systems introduce second-order regulation precisely when local processing fails to guarantee stability. This observation, taken alone, is descriptive. What the formal framework adds is necessity: such regulation is not simply advantageous, it becomes required once coherence is threatened.

The convergence is therefore asymmetric. The formal demonstration of coherence does not depend on neuroscientific data for its validity. It is established independently as a condition of possibility for persistence. Empirical data, by contrast, progressively encounter this constraint as system complexity increases and experimental resolution improves.

This asymmetry explains a recurring pattern in cognitive science. Higher-order constructs—confidence, monitoring, awareness, abstraction—are repeatedly rediscovered, redescribed, and localized in the brain. Yet their role remains ambiguous as long as they are treated as properties rather than operators. Once coherence is recognized as the governing constraint, their function becomes unambiguous: they are mechanisms that temporarily restore compatibility across states.

From this viewpoint, empirical neuroscience has reached a boundary condition. It can continue to refine correlates and mechanisms, but it cannot explain why second-order regulation appears without invoking a prior constraint. That constraint is coherence.

The phrase the empirical meets the formal does not indicate a synthesis of two theories. It describes a limit process: empirical investigation converges toward a mathematical necessity it did not set out to discover. The proof was not derived from the data; the data arrived at the proof.

In this sense, recent neuroscientific results do not open a new theoretical space. They close one. They mark the point at which empirical explanation reaches the edge of its own admissibility and must be situated within a formal framework that was already there.

## 8. Conclusion
### A Boundary, Not a Hypothesis

This white paper has advanced a single claim: recent empirical results in cognitive neuroscience converge toward a formal constraint that was established independently of those results. Metacognition, abstract structural representation, and conscious regulation are not foundational features of cognition; they are conditional operators activated when coherence is threatened.

The contribution of empirical neuroscience is decisive, but limited. It identifies where second-order regulation appears and how it operates. What it cannot do—by design—is explain why such regulation becomes necessary. That explanation belongs to a prior level of analysis, where persistence, compatibility, and coherence define the conditions under which any system can continue to exist as itself.

From this perspective, the relationship between data and theory is not one of mutual construction. The formal demonstration of coherence does not await empirical validation, nor do the data derive their meaning from the formalism. The convergence is one-directional: as empirical investigation reaches its own limits, it encounters a constraint that was already there.

This does not diminish the value of empirical work. On the contrary, it clarifies its scope. The role of neuroscience is not to discover the foundations of coherence, but to reveal how and when biological systems are forced to comply with it.

In this sense, the present convergence marks an endpoint rather than a beginning. It closes a theoretical space in which higher-order cognition could be treated as either foundational or accidental. What remains is a coherent hierarchy: first-order processes suffice when coherence is locally maintained; second-order regulation appears when it is not; consciousness emerges when global arbitration becomes unavoidable.

The empirical did not refute the formal.
It arrived there.
