Coherence as a Transversal Invariant of Admissibility
Why Multiple Foundational Problems Stall at the Same Structural Boundary

ABSTRACT
This white paper proposes a transversal analysis of ten foundational problems traditionally regarded as unsolved or structurally blocked in mathematics and physics. Although these problems differ widely in formulation, domain, scale, and historical context, they exhibit a common failure mode. In each case, formal frameworks remain locally valid and mathematically consistent, yet global continuation becomes possible only through unbounded divergence, infinite or non-traceable compensation, loss of traceability, or descriptive breakdown. The obstruction is therefore not formal solvability, but admissibility.

To explain this convergence, the paper introduces coherence as a minimal invariant of second order governing admissible persistence. Coherence constrains whether a descriptive trajectory can persist without internal contradiction, unbounded divergence, or non-closed compensation. Defined independently of equations, scales, or physical interpretation, this invariant is applied uniformly, without modification, across ten foundational problems.

This work does not claim explicit solutions, closed-form constructions, or Clay-level proofs. It requalifies insolubility as a consequence of missing admissibility constraints and clarifies what kind of progress remains possible beyond the structural boundary where formal methods systematically stall.

INTRODUCTION
Over the past decades, mathematics and theoretical physics have produced an extraordinary number of formally well-defined equations, models, and computational frameworks. Yet across multiple foundational domains, problems that are rigorously formulated and intensely studied continue to resist definitive resolution.

In each case, trajectories or constructions remain formally computable while exhibiting unbounded divergence, loss of traceability, dependence on infinite compensation, or descriptive breakdown. These phenomena mark a boundary beyond which formal evolution no longer corresponds to an admissible persistent description.

The central thesis of this paper is that many problems regarded as insoluble do not fail because of missing techniques or insufficient formal power, but because they implicitly cross a boundary of admissibility that is never made explicit. This boundary is governed by a missing invariant: coherence.

DEFINITION OF COHERENCE
A system is coherent if its evolution can persist without internal contradiction, without unbounded divergence, and without requiring infinite or non-traceable compensation. Coherence is an invariant of second order: it constrains admissibility rather than state evolution.

Inadmissibility here is epistemological, not ontological. A solution may remain mathematically valid while being descriptively inadmissible within a coherent modeling framework.

The coherence criterion is falsifiable. Any trajectory violating coherence while remaining globally admissible without compensation would invalidate the framework.

TEN PROBLEMS ANALYZED UNDER COHERENCE
1. Navier–Stokes: blow-up regimes violate coherence through unbounded gradients.
2. Yang–Mills: absence of mass gap violates coherence via unbounded spectral accumulation.
3. General Relativity: singularities violate coherence through loss of causal traceability.
4. Riemann Hypothesis: off-line zeros violate coherence via unbounded error amplification.
5. Turbulence: unbounded cascades violate coherence through loss of closure.
6. Renormalization (QFT): infinite non-closed compensation violates coherence.
7. N-body chaos: infinite precision requirements violate coherence.
8. Arrow of Time: perfect reversibility violates coherence by erasing traceability.
9. Quantum Measurement: lack of irreversible trace violates coherence.
10. Cosmology: singular origins violate coherence as descriptive boundaries.

CONVERGENCE
Across all ten problems, formal frameworks remain locally valid while global admissibility collapses at the same structural boundary. Coherence governs this boundary as a transversal invariant.

FINAL CONCLUSION
Coherence does not replace mathematics. It conditions the domain in which mathematical descriptions can persist without contradiction, divergence, or non-closed compensation. Insolubility is thereby reframed from a technical limitation to a structural boundary.
